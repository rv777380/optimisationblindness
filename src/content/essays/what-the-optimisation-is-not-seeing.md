---
title: "What the Optimisation Is Not Seeing"
date: 2026-05-19
description: "On Marc Priestley's question to leadership teams and what it cannot quite reach in AI-mediated tourism cities."
---

I read something Marc Priestley wrote recently about Formula One, and it stayed with me longer than it probably should have. His question to leadership teams is "What are you optimising for?", and he describes how the same F1 car, the same team, the same driver will be set up completely differently for Saturday's qualifying lap than for Sunday's race. One game is a single perfect lap. The other is two hours of accumulation. Same machine. Different optimisation.

I read that on a flight back from a small Mediterranean city I had been working in. I had spent three days there. I had seen the same six restaurants recommended on three different platforms, watched the same itinerary surface on the city's marketing app, the city's destination app, and the city's hotel concierge tablet. I had eaten well. The trip had gone smoothly. And something about it had felt thin in a way I could not name, until I read Priestley's question and realised that nobody in that city had been asked it.

In F1, the team chooses what to optimise for, and that choice is conscious. They argue about it. They model it. They know that downforce makes them faster through corners but slower on the straights. They know what they are trading.

In a tourism city, we have the inverse problem. Multiple systems are optimising in parallel, and no one is choosing on behalf of the destination as a whole. The revenue management system is optimising yield. The recommendation platform is optimising engagement. The mobility optimiser is balancing flows. The pricing engine is squeezing willingness to pay at the moments visitors have the least flexibility. Each is locally rational. Each is doing exactly what it was asked to do.

What none of them is asked is what is happening to the city.

I have come to call this optimisation blindness. The phrase is meant literally. It is not that the systems are wrong. It is that the signals which would tell them something is wrong are absent from their objective function. A recommendation system optimised for engagement will produce engagement. It will not notice that visitors are converging onto the same handful of places while the rest of the city becomes invisible to the mediated visitor. A pricing engine will not notice that the guest paying €420 on a Tuesday felt extracted from rather than welcomed. A mobility platform will not flag the neighbourhoods that used to define the city and now go unseen. The thing the city is becoming is happening below the resolution of the instruments.

Priestley's question is essential. It just does not quite reach this problem.

In F1, optimisation is deliberate, sequenced, and owned. In a tourism city, optimisation is continuous, distributed across dozens of systems, and unowned. The question is therefore not only *what are you optimising for*. The question, increasingly, is *what is the optimisation not seeing*.

Those are different questions. The first is a choice. The second is a blind spot.

I will not pretend to a tidy resolution. I have been working on a measurement architecture, the [Destination Emotional Quality Index](/deqi), currently under review at the *International Journal of Tourism Cities*, to make the blind spot visible. And on a governance mechanism, destination-level circuit breakers, to give cities a way to act on what they find. Both will take time. The argument is being shaped, peer-reviewed, contested. That is how it should be.

What I can already say with some confidence is this. The destinations that hold their character through the next decade will not be the ones with the most sophisticated AI stack. They will be the ones whose leadership keeps asking, at every level, what the optimisation is not telling them. The signal underneath the dashboard. The guest the system did not flag. The neighbourhood the algorithm forgot.

The F1 team that wins on Sunday is not always the one with the fastest lap on Saturday.

The tourism city that still feels like itself in ten years will not be the one that optimised hardest. It will be the one that noticed, in time, what optimisation could not.

— Rui Vasconcelos
